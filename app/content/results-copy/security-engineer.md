## What this role actually is

Security Engineer covers designing security architecture, running risk and vulnerability assessments, and — increasingly — partnering directly with product and engineering teams to build security into the software development lifecycle rather than gatekeeping it at the end. The umbrella splits into real sub-specializations (cloud security, application security, network/infrastructure security, detection & response) that hire somewhat differently, but they share a common core. The "security cop who says no to everything" stereotype is largely wrong for how the job is actually described in postings: the highest-leverage work is collaborative — consulting with developers, educating teams on secure coding, and being judged on whether people actually adopt the guardrails you build, not just whether the guardrails exist. It's also not primarily offensive hacking; most of the profession is defense, incident response, risk management, and architecture.

## A day in this role

Expect a mix of hands-on and advisory work: reviewing architecture for a new service with an eye toward exploitable weaknesses, triaging and tracking remediation of vulnerability findings, and consulting with a product team on secure-by-default patterns before code ships rather than after. Detection-and-response-leaning roles spend more time building and tuning detection systems and owning live incident response when something does go wrong — parallel to SRE's incident work but scoped to security events specifically. Application-security-leaning roles look more like embedded partnership: pairing with developers, writing rules for tools like Semgrep, and being judged on developer adoption of what you build. Staying current on the threat landscape and translating compliance regimes (SOC 2, PCI DSS, NIST) into concrete engineering asks is a recurring undercurrent regardless of specialization.

## Comp structure

Base-heavy with equity, tracking general software engineering comp discipline rather than a separate security pay scale. Reported medians run roughly $240K-$300K at large tech companies (Google, Amazon, Nvidia, Cisco), with a real posted Staff+ range at a frontier AI lab of $320K-$485K. Entry-level security work (SOC analyst and similar feeder roles, not yet titled "security engineer") pays far less — commonly $70K-$85K — and the realistic path to "security engineer" titling and the higher bands above typically takes 5-8 years, not a fast bootcamp-to-six-figures jump.

## How to test this cheaply

Pick a system you already know well (a side project, or a service at work you have access to) and run a real threat-modeling pass on it — write down concrete attack paths, not just a generic checklist — and notice whether that exercise energizes you or feels like a chore. Separately, most security-engineering paths run through a SOC-analyst or application-security on-ramp; shadowing a SOC analyst's shift or pairing with an AppSec engineer on a code review for a day is a low-cost way to see the entry-level reality (queue-driven triage, developer consultations) before committing to the multi-year path toward the senior comp figures above.
