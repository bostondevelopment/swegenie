## What this role actually is

Developer Relations / Developer Advocacy is public-facing technical content and community work — not marketing wearing an engineer's job title, whatever the reporting line says. The role spans four recurring buckets: technical content (blog posts, tutorials, sample apps, video), public speaking, community engagement (forums, meetups, Discord/Discord-adjacent moderation), and feeding developer friction back to product/engineering. It's explicitly not tied to accounts or tickets — success is measured by community and ecosystem health, not case resolution or account expansion — and DevRel practitioners are emphatic that hiring a marketer and calling them "Developer Advocate" gets seen through by developers immediately.

## A day in this role

Your week mixes planned content production — drafting a tutorial, recording a demo video, writing a blog post based on real product usage — with community engagement: answering questions in a Discord or on Stack Overflow, moderating a forum, or running office hours that surface friction logs for product and engineering. Some weeks include travel for a conference talk or workshop; others are almost entirely remote content work, depending on how your specific role is weighted (a "Developer Experience Engineer" flavor skews toward SDKs/docs/CLI improvement with more code and less travel; a "Community" flavor skews toward events with less hands-on coding). You'll build sample apps and proofs-of-concept to demonstrate integration patterns, but that's in service of the content and community work, not the main deliverable. A recurring, real frustration: no one metric your organization trusts actually captures whether you're succeeding.

## Comp structure

Base plus equity, with little to no sales-style variable comp — a structural difference from Sales/Solutions Engineering. Levels.fyi shows a median Developer Advocate salary around $156K across companies, rising sharply at big tech (Google $190K-$376K depending on level, median $217K; Amazon $165K-$236K, median $190K). The single biggest swing factor isn't company size — it's whether the role reports into engineering or marketing, which one compensation analysis says can set the pay band "before anything else." Discretionary annual bonuses exist; commission-style deal-linked pay does not.

## How to test this cheaply

Give a lightning talk at a local meetup or write one public tutorial for a tool you actually use, then honestly ask yourself whether you'd want to do it again next month — the recurring, sustained cadence is what separates DevRel from an engineer who occasionally enjoys writing a blog post. Separately, spend a week answering questions in a public developer community (a product's Discord, a subreddit, Stack Overflow) and notice whether "translating for a stranger who wasn't in the room" energizes you or feels like a tax on real work — that's the job's daily texture, distilled.
