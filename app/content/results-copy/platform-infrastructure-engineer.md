## What this role actually is

A Platform / Infrastructure Engineer builds and operates the internal developer platform — self-service infra, golden paths, CI/CD, provisioning APIs — that lets product teams ship without needing deep infra expertise themselves. The key distinction from "rebranded DevOps": DevOps solves one team's pipeline problem, platform engineering solves the same class of problem at organizational scale, as a reusable product other engineers adopt. It's easy to mistake this for a pure ops/infra-code job — it isn't. Multiple sources are blunt that this is as much a product-mindset job as a technical one, and engineers who just want to write infra code in isolation, with no requirements-gathering or internal advocacy, tend to be a poor fit despite the technical overlap.

## A day in this role

You'll manage cloud infrastructure and IaC (Terraform, Pulumi), operate Kubernetes clusters, and write client libraries other engineers pull directly into their own services — real production code, not just config. A meaningful chunk of your week goes to requirements-gathering: meeting with internal "customer" teams, defining what the platform should do next, and iterating based on their feedback rather than executing a top-down mandate. At data-heavy orgs this specializes into data-platform work; at smaller companies (sub-500 engineers) this role frequently blends with SRE work, so read the actual responsibilities section of any posting, not just the title. Success is measured by adoption and whether product engineers are shipping faster with less infra knowledge required — a technically elegant platform nobody uses is a failure by this role's own standard.

## Comp structure

Base-heavy with equity, mirroring general software engineering comp — there's no material sales-style variable comp here. Reported 2025 US median base is roughly $172K with median total comp around $235K at mid-career, rising past $400K total at Staff level; Bay Area runs higher (median base ~$198K, total ~$310K). AI-infrastructure-adjacent platform roles currently command a premium, roughly $180K-$310K at mid-to-senior levels. There's no separate "platform engineer" comp track on levels.fyi distinct from general senior SWE bands — it's priced as a software engineering specialization, not its own discipline.

## How to test this cheaply

Find a "platform engineering" or "developer platform" team at a company you know and ask to sit in on one of their internal roadmap or requirements-gathering meetings with a consuming team — that conversation is the actual job in miniature. Alternatively, try building a small internal tool (a shared CI template, a self-service provisioning script) for a team you're not on and see whether the requirements-gathering and adoption-chasing part of it energizes you or feels like a tax on the "real" engineering work.
