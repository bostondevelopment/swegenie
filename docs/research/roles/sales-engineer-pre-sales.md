# Sales Engineer / Pre-Sales Engineer

**Also known as:** Solutions Engineer (many SaaS companies use this title interchangeably with
Sales Engineer — see Naming Note), Presales Engineer, Technical Sales Engineer.

## Naming note

"Sales Engineer" and "Solutions Engineer" are largely the same job at most B2B software
companies — the title is a company-culture choice, not a role distinction (e.g., Salesforce and
many SaaS companies say "Solutions Engineer" to soften the "sales" association, while
infrastructure/devtools companies like Perforce more often say "Sales Engineer"). This brief
treats them as one archetype. Where "Solutions Engineer" and "Solutions Architect" diverge is
scope and seniority, not the sales-vs-non-sales axis — see the Solutions Architect briefs for that
boundary.

## Day-to-day activities

- Partners with an Account Executive as the technical half of a two-person deal team: the AE owns
  the relationship/commercial terms, the SE owns technical validation and proves the product will
  actually work for the prospect ([Sales Engineers of New York — SWE-to-SE transition
  post](https://www.salesengineersny.com/blog/transitioning-from-a-software-engineer-to-a-sales-engineer)).
- Runs discovery calls to understand a prospect's technical environment, pain points, and buying
  criteria; asks business-impact questions, not just technical ones (same source).
- Delivers live and customized product demos (remote or onsite), tailored to the prospect's stack
  and use case rather than a canned script.
- Writes RFP/RFI technical responses; maintains demo environments/databases; stays current on
  product features and competitive positioning ([Upwork Sales Engineer job description
  template](https://www.upwork.com/hire/sales-engineers/job-description/); [Coursera — What Is a
  Sales Engineer?](https://www.coursera.org/articles/sales-engineer)).
- Runs Proof-of-Concept (POC) engagements: scoping success criteria with the customer, standing up
  a trial environment, and reporting results — this is where the "technical win" is actually
  earned, distinct from the AE's "business win."
- Positions against competitors technically (feature-for-feature, architecture-for-architecture),
  not just on price/relationship ([Censys Senior Sales Engineer
  posting](https://boards.greenhouse.io/censys/jobs/7050361002)).
- Feeds structured customer/market feedback back to product management — a channel individual
  contributors on the AE side don't have.
- Travel and onsite presence vary widely by deal size — enterprise SEs may be on planes weekly for
  finalist presentations; SMB/mid-market SEs may be almost entirely remote/video-call based.

## Success criteria (comp structure is the defining feature)

**This is the single biggest differentiator vs. almost every other engineering archetype: SEs are
paid substantially on variable/commission tied to their team's sales attainment, not project
delivery or code shipped.**

- Standard SE comp is a 70/30 or 80/20 base-to-variable split, with 80/20 most common at SaaS
  companies. A $175K OTE SE earns roughly $140K base + $35K at-quota variable
  ([Everstage — Sales Engineer Compensation
  2026](https://www.everstage.com/sales-compensation/sales-engineer-compensation)).
- Variable pay is typically tied to *team* or *territory* quota attainment (not an individual quota
  the way an AE has one) — the SE is rewarded for the deals their aligned AE(s) close, which
  means success is measured jointly, not solely on individual technical output.
- Formal success metrics used by SE leadership commonly include: win rate on deals where the SE
  was engaged, POC-to-close conversion rate, technical-win attach rate, and AE satisfaction/demand
  for that SE (peer-reported norm across the sources above; also see [We The Sales Engineers — SE
  comp structure](https://wethesalesengineers.com/salarystructure/)).
- This variable-comp structure is the key thing that should NOT be conflated with Forward-Deployed
  Engineer or Consulting SA in the assessment's incentive dimension — SE income has direct
  quarterly volatility tied to deals; FDE and most consulting-SA comp does not.

## Career ladder

Typical progression (synthesized across postings and practitioner sources):

1. Associate/Jr. Sales Engineer (SMB or mid-market territory)
2. Sales Engineer (mid-market/commercial)
3. Senior Sales Engineer (enterprise accounts, larger deal sizes, mentors juniors)
4. Principal/Staff Sales Engineer or Lead SE (strategic accounts, sets technical POC methodology,
   often the escalation point for the hardest technical objections)
5. Branch points from Principal SE: (a) SE Manager/Director → sales engineering leadership track,
   (b) Sales (AE) — some SEs convert to full quota-carrying sales roles, (c) Product Management,
   (d) Solutions Architect (post-sale, deeper implementation focus), (e) individual-contributor
   "field CTO" style roles at the VP+ level for strategic accounts only.

([Rainmakers — Sales Engineer Career Path](https://www.rainmakers.co/blog/sales-engineer-career-path/);
[Springboard — Sales Engineer Career Path Guide 2025](https://www.springboard.com/blog/sales/sales-engineer-career-path/))

## Common entry paths

- **Lateral from Software Engineering** — the most relevant path for this product's audience.
  Engineers who enjoy demoing their work, explaining systems to non-engineers, and want more
  variety/less heads-down time self-select in. No prior sales experience is typically required;
  companies expect to train the sales methodology on top of existing technical credibility
  ([Sales Engineers of New York](https://www.salesengineersny.com/blog/transitioning-from-a-software-engineer-to-a-sales-engineer)
  — author reports landing an SE role at AppDynamics with zero prior sales experience).
- Implementation consultants / customer success engineers moving into presales, bringing deep
  product/domain knowledge and a customer-facing skill set already built.
- Support engineers or solutions consultants moving "up funnel" into presales.
- New-grad or early-career hires directly into SE associate programs at large vendors (less common
  than lateral moves, but exists at companies like Salesforce, ServiceNow).

## Common exit paths

- Account Executive / full quota-carrying sales (natural adjacent move given shared deal-cycle
  fluency).
- Sales Engineering management/leadership.
- Product Management (SEs accumulate structured, first-hand customer/market feedback that PM
  hiring managers value).
- Solutions Architect or Customer Success/Professional Services (post-sale technical roles).
- Founder/entrepreneurship — SEs who've seen many companies' sales motions and technical gaps
  sometimes spin out startups (noted directionally in [Rainmakers career path
  piece](https://www.rainmakers.co/blog/sales-engineer-career-path/); anecdotal, not
  statistically sourced — flagged as an open question below).

## Top misconceptions vs. reality

1. **"It's not real engineering, it's just demos and slides."** Reality: SEs run live technical
   discovery, design POC architectures against a prospect's actual (often messy) environment,
   debug live failures in front of the customer with the AE and the deal on the line, and write
   technical RFP responses that get reviewed by the prospect's own engineers. The technical bar to
   be credible in front of a skeptical staff engineer at the prospect company is high — you cannot
   fake your way through a live technical objection. ([helppeoplebuy.com — "Can engineers sell
   without losing technical credibility?"](https://helppeoplebuy.com/answers/can-engineers-learn-to-sell-without-losing-technical-credibility/)
   argues directly against this stereotype: "Technical buyers respect directness, systematic
   thinking, and evidence-based reasoning — engineering qualities that apply to commercial
   conversations and make you more credible, not less.")
2. **"You need to already be a salesperson / be extroverted-pushy to do this."** Reality: multiple
   sources report SEs with zero sales background succeeding by leaning on technical credibility
   and structured discovery rather than classic sales charisma; the job rewards being a trusted,
   evidence-based technical advisor more than being a "closer" ([Sales Engineer Training — "Top 7
   Reasons Engineers are Afraid to Become Sales
   Engineers"](https://www.salesengineertraining.com/sales-engineer-role-afraid)).
3. **"SEs just deliver a canned deck."** Reality: much of the job is live, improvised technical
   problem-solving under audience pressure — closer to live debugging with an audience than
   scripted presenting ([MarketStar — "4 Misperceptions About Sales
   Engineers"](https://marketstar.com/impact-blog/4-misperceptions-about-sales-engineers)).
4. **"Comp is basically a stable engineer salary with a small bonus."** Reality: variable pay is a
   real, non-trivial share of OTE (20–30%) tied to team/territory attainment — income has genuine
   quarter-to-quarter variance unlike most SWE comp, which is the central risk/reward trade this
   role makes vs. an IC engineering track.
