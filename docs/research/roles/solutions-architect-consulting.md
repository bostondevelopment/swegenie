# Role Archetype Research Brief: Solutions Architect — Consulting-side

**Cluster:** Pre-Sales & Client-Facing Technical (siblings: Sales Engineer, Forward-Deployed Engineer — see separate briefs)
**Scope:** Systems integrator / consultancy SA or technical architect who works across many vendors' products for client engagements (e.g., Accenture, Deloitte Digital, Slalom, Capgemini, Infosys, TCS, boutique SIs) — as distinct from a vendor-employed SA (AWS/Salesforce/Google Cloud) who is deep on one product stack.

---

## 1. Day-to-day activities

Consulting-side SAs split time across **pre-sale (bid/proposal) work and delivery work**, often for multiple concurrent clients or projects rather than one long embed:

- **Solutioning and design across the client lifecycle.** Job postings describe leading "technical discussions, solutioning workshops, and architectural reviews with clients and internal stakeholders," designing end-to-end technology solutions aligned to client requirements, and evaluating current systems for improvement opportunities [1]. Deloitte postings describe environment provisioning, cluster governance, security/access control, and cost optimization "across multiple environments," explicitly spanning many client environments and cloud/tooling stacks rather than one product [2].
- **RFP and proposal work.** Solutions architects are described as playing "a central role in the RFP process," including a discovery phase with client stakeholders to establish project boundaries, timeline, and budget before a statement of work is signed [8]. Providing "detailed level of effort estimates for proposed solutions" is a named Slalom SA responsibility [3].
- **Discovery workshops and requirements translation.** Practitioner Florian Hoeppner (self-described "value architect," writing from a systems-integrator/consulting vantage point) describes the job as translating client priorities (cost reduction, flexibility, service quality) into a value proposition, then mapping solution components — "as if Lego bricks" — to that proposition, acting as an alignment point between the bid team and the client throughout proposal and delivery phases [9].
- **Cross-vendor / cross-platform integration.** Capgemini SA postings list producing "solution blueprints, sequence diagrams, integration models, and network topology models," engaging "external vendors and internal platform teams for integration," and acting as "technical authority in workshops, steering committees, and delivery squads" — language implying multiple vendors/platforms per engagement, not one product line [4]. Industry commentary contrasts this directly with vendor SAs: consulting/enterprise-facing SAs need "breadth across platforms, integration technologies, cloud services, enterprise patterns," useful when "your environment is mixed, changing, or not yet locked into one provider" [11].
- **Multi-client/multi-project portfolio and utilization pressure.** Utilization (billable hours ÷ available hours) is the central operating metric for consulting staff, with industry benchmarks of 75–85% for staff-level consultants and lower (30–70%) for directors/partners who shift toward business development [6]. This structurally implies juggling a portfolio of billable engagements rather than one dedicated deployment.
- **Travel.** Accenture and "most other Big Four firms" are reported to carry 50%+ travel requirements for consulting staff, a contrast noted explicitly against Slalom, which matches local teams to local clients and reports comparatively less travel [10].

## 2. Success criteria / how performance is measured

- **Utilization rate** is the primary, named lever: (billable hours / available hours) × 100, with typical firm thresholds around 1,600–1,800 annual billable hours; a 10-point utilization increase is estimated to add roughly $150K in annual revenue per consultant at a $150/hr rate — illustrating why utilization is tracked so closely as an individual performance metric in consulting firms [6]. One practitioner-oriented piece specifically cautions that firms are moving toward tying bonuses to "utilization and project margins together," not raw hours alone, and that some firms tie up to 30% of bonus to non-billable activities like training completion — evidence that pure utilization is an incomplete but still dominant proxy [6].
- **Delivery/client satisfaction and business development contribution** feed promotion decisions alongside utilization: consulting promotion up the ladder (project manager → principal → director → partner) is "influenced by a consultant's performance, their ability to forge strong client relationships, and their contributions to business development" [7].
- **Up-or-out promotion structure** is real but not universal across all consulting-model firms. At MBB and Big 4 firms, "up or out" is a named, formal HR policy: consultants are promoted on a set timeline or exited; MBB analyst-to-partner runs ~10–12 years, Big 4 (Deloitte, EY, PwC, KPMG) closer to 13–17 years given more intermediate levels [7]. By contrast, Slalom is reported to explicitly **not** run an up-or-out policy, allowing "long-term careers without feeling forced to leave" — a useful counter-data-point showing the model is firm-dependent, not a defining trait of every consulting-side SA employer [10]. This nuance matters for the taxonomy: "up-or-out" should be described as common/typical at large SIs and Big 4, not universal to the whole archetype.

## 3. Comp structure

Base + bonus is the norm; equity is rare below partner/MD level (consistent with these being private partnerships or, for public firms like Accenture, broad-based RSU programs rather than sales-style variable comp).

- **Accenture (US), Solution Architect, levels.fyi, updated June 2026:** Associate Solution Architect $115K to Team Leader $169K; median total comp across the Solution Architect ladder ~$170K. Senior Solution Architect median total comp $196,060 = base $180,960 + stock $3,600/yr + bonus $11,500. Top reported package: $339,000/yr [12]. Note the bonus component here (~$11.5K on a ~$181K base, about 6%) is modest relative to a sales-commission structure, consistent with the brief's premise that consulting SA bonus is not typically commission-driven — though levels.fyi doesn't label the bonus as utilization-linked explicitly, so that specific causal link is *inferred*, not directly confirmed by this source (see Open Questions).
- **Deloitte, Solutions Architect / Solution Architect:**
  - Glassdoor (675 salaries, as of ~May 2026): average $217,330/yr; 25th–75th percentile $175,843–$272,750 [13].
  - Comparably: average $190,000/yr = base $165,000 + bonus $25,000; 80% of Deloitte Solutions Architects report receiving an annual bonus [13].
  - Levels.fyi (Greater Dallas): $122K–$227K+ [13].
  - 6figr: $136K–$338K range [10]; a separate 6figr pull cites average base $167K, average total comp $186K, range $136K–$335K across 91 reported profiles, with Senior Solution Architects averaging $175,607 [19].
  - These sources vary by ~$70–100K at the high end depending on level/geography mix, which is expected given "Solutions Architect" spans Consultant through Senior Manager-equivalent levels at Deloitte.
- **Slalom, Solutions Architect:** estimated total pay range $174K–$222K/yr, average base ~$180K/yr [3].
- **Cross-check (Glassdoor, Accenture Solutions Architect):** a separate Glassdoor pull reports a wider band than the Levels.fyi figures above — $182,596–$323,244/yr across seniority, Senior Solution Architects averaging ~$287,532 and up to ~$466,848 at the 90th percentile [19]. The spread between this and the Levels.fyi Accenture figures in this section is large enough to flag as a reconciliation gap (see Open Questions) rather than resolve by picking one source.
- **General senior-level market band (Glassdoor aggregate, cross-firm):** average $225,767/yr, typical range $183,894–$281,737 (25th–75th percentile) [19].
- **Thoughtworks:** seniormost Solution/System Architect roles require 10+ years in technology with 5+ years specifically in consulting and a track record leading application or data practices in consulting or enterprise environments; specific comp figures were not independently confirmed by either research pass on this archetype — flagged as a genuine gap, not just a single-source weakness [19].
- **Bonus structure vs. sales commission — verification status:** Direct confirmation that consulting-SA bonuses are formally tied to utilization/billability (rather than structured as sales commission) comes from the utilization-bonus literature [6] and the general consulting-promotion literature [7], both of which describe utilization and margin as the bonus-driving levers for delivery staff, distinct from a percent-of-deal-value commission structure typical of vendor-side / quota-carrying sales engineering roles. None of the specific company salary aggregators (Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Comparably) explicitly label the bonus line as "utilization-linked" — this is a **plausible, industry-literature-supported inference**, not a directly-cited company policy document, and should be flagged as such in the taxonomy (see Open Questions).
- **Equity:** Not mentioned in any SA-level posting or comp source found; consistent with equity being reserved for partner/Managing Director level at these firms, which is standard knowledge for partnership-structured consultancies but was not separately verified with a citation in this research pass.

## 4. Career ladder

Composite ladder synthesized from job postings and consulting-career-path sources, with real title examples:

- **Entry/Associate:** "Associate Solution Architect" (Accenture, per levels.fyi) [12]; broader consulting entry titles are Analyst/Associate Consultant [7].
- **Individual contributor architect:** "Solution Architect," "Solution Architect (Senior Consultant)" — this exact title pairing appears in a live Deloitte posting, showing Deloitte maps "Solution Architect" to the "Senior Consultant" band [5]. Also: "Solution Architect, Deputy Manager" (Deloitte USI) [5], "Technology Architect" (Accenture) [1].
- **Senior IC / people-leadership hybrid:** "Senior Solution Architect" (Accenture, levels.fyi: median total comp $196K) [12]; "Team Leader" band at Accenture tops the SA-specific ladder at $169K base before moving into broader management titles [12].
- **Management track:** "Solution Architect Manager" (live Accenture posting title) [1]; consulting-wide this maps to Manager → Senior Manager → Director, per the general promotion-timeline literature [7].
- **Executive/Partner track:** Director → Partner/Managing Director. Partner-track promotion is gated less by raw performance and more by "a proven book of business and strong client relationships" — described as the single biggest promotion bottleneck in consulting [7]. Slalom's ladder culminates in "Managing Directors who serve as office or practice leaders" [10].
- Titles are **not standardized across firms** — Accenture, Deloitte, Slalom, Capgemini, and Infosys each use "Solution Architect" / "Solutions Architect" / "Technology Architect" somewhat interchangeably and map them to different internal consultant bands, which is itself evidence worth flagging for the taxonomy (title alone is a weak signal; level + firm context is needed).

## 5. Common entry paths

Not deeply covered by the sources gathered in this pass (gap — see Open Questions), but inferable from job posting qualification lists:
- Lateral hire from software engineering/technical delivery roles into a client-facing SA role, based on postings requiring "hands-on experience with enterprise and/or application architecture" [2] and platform-specific technical depth (e.g., SAP EWM, Oracle Cloud, Teamcenter, O9 — Infosys posts many single-platform "Solution Architect" openings under the SA title, suggesting a common path is deep IC expertise in one platform first, generalized into a consulting SA role) [14].
- Internal promotion from Consultant/Senior Consultant within the same firm, evidenced by the Deloitte "Solution Architect (Senior Consultant)" title pairing indicating SA is reached via the standard consulting ladder rather than being a lateral-only entry point [5].
- Lateral hire directly from a specific enterprise-platform IC background (e.g., a Workday, Salesforce, or SAP consultant who becomes a Solution Architect specializing in that platform) — a pattern called out explicitly for boutique consultancies like Slalom [19].
- Lateral hire from vendor-side SA or Sales Engineer roles, bringing single-platform expertise into a multi-vendor advisory context [19].
- Explicitly *not deeply verified in this pass*: MBA/campus-hire entry, bootcamp-to-consulting pipelines, or specific "typical prior title" statistics. Flagged as an open question.

## 6. Common exit paths

Not directly evidenced by sources in this pass — this is a **gap**, not a documented finding for most of the commonly-assumed destinations. One indirect data point: a Fishbowl (anonymous professional forum) thread comparing "Slalom vs Accenture for tech strategy consulting" for resume value and "future exit opps" confirms that exit-opportunity comparison is a live, discussed topic among practitioners, but the thread content itself (specific destinations) was not extracted in this pass [10].

A second, independent research pass on this same archetype did surface directional (pattern-recognition, not statistically quantified) evidence for several commonly-assumed exits, which is included here with that caveat carried forward [19]:
- **Vendor-side Solutions Architect** — moving from multi-vendor advisory into single-vendor advocacy, often cited informally as trading breadth for better work-life predictability and equity upside.
- **In-house Enterprise Architect or Staff/Principal Engineer at a former client** — a "consultant impresses a client and gets poached into a permanent role" pattern, described as well-documented anecdotally but not independently backed by a statistic in either research pass.
- **Consulting leadership track** (Principal → Partner) for those staying in consulting long-term — this is the ladder progression already documented in Section 4, not a true "exit."
- **Independent/boutique consulting**, or founding a niche systems-integration practice, leveraging cross-client pattern recognition.
- **Product management at a platform vendor** — e.g., a former Workday or Salesforce implementation architect moving into PM at that vendor — flagged explicitly as a recognized pattern rather than a quantified statistic; treat as the weakest-evidenced exit-path claim in this section.

None of the above five bullets should be treated as more than directional until a dedicated follow-up (LinkedIn bio survey or a consulting-exit-opportunities dataset) confirms them.

## 7. Top misconceptions vs. reality

- **Misconception: consulting SAs are glorified project managers who don't touch technology.**
  Reality: Thoughtworks' own job requirements explicitly mandate that Solution Architects
  "continue to code alongside developers," applying TDD, pairing, CI, and infrastructure
  automation — a primary-source counter-example from a job requirement, not a marketing claim
  [19]. The project-management layer documented elsewhere in this brief is additive on top of
  real technical depth, not a replacement for it.
- **Misconception: "Solutions Architect" means the same job everywhere.** Reality: the title is used inconsistently even within consulting — Infosys applies "Solution Architect" to deep single-platform roles (Teamcenter SA, Oracle Cloud Supply Chain SA, O9 SA) [14], while Capgemini and Slalom use it for broader integration/delivery-leadership roles [3][4]. Level and firm context matter more than the title string.
- **Misconception: consulting SA comp is commission-driven like sales.** Reality: available comp data shows base salary + a modest annual bonus (Accenture ~6% of base; Deloitte bonus commonly cited around $25K on ~$165K base, i.e., ~15%) [12][13], structurally different from percentage-of-deal-value sales commission. The literature on consulting bonus structure ties this to utilization/margin rather than individual deal-closing, though as noted in Section 3 this causal link is supported by industry literature rather than a specific company policy document [6].
- **Misconception: all consulting SAs are on a rigid "up or out" clock.** Reality: this is true at MBB and Big 4-style firms but explicitly not true everywhere — Slalom is documented as not running an up-or-out policy [10], so "up-or-out" should be scoped as common-but-not-universal within this archetype.
- **Misconception: consulting SA = pre-sales only, no real technical depth.** Reality: postings show deep, hands-on technical requirements (Kubernetes, Elastic Search, cluster governance, IaC, AWS/Java/SAP specializations) [1][2][4][14] — the role is not purely a bid-support/slideware function; it carries real delivery accountability, distinguishing it from a pure Sales Engineer role (though the two clusters likely overlap at smaller firms — not independently verified here).
- **Misconception: travel/lifestyle is uniform across "consulting SA."** Reality: explicitly firm-dependent — Big Four-style 50%+ travel vs. Slalom's local-team/local-client model, with correspondingly different work-life-balance reputations [10].

## 8. Consulting-side vs. vendor-side AND vs. forward-deployed-engineer: distinguishing evidence

This is the section that most directly feeds the split-vs-merge taxonomy decision. Evidence found:

### vs. Vendor-side SA (AWS/Salesforce/Google Cloud-style)

| Dimension | Consulting-side SA | Vendor-side SA | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Breadth vs. depth | Needs "breadth across platforms, integration technologies, cloud services, enterprise patterns" | "Expert in how to apply a particular platform to a specific problem, deep diving into detailed implementation discussions," with "solutions being pre-defined but fine-tuned" | Direct sourced contrast: "vendor architects specialize deeply in optimizing solutions within a specific platform, while consulting architects maintain broader knowledge to address diverse business problems across multiple technology stacks and vendors" [11] |
| Employer type | Employed by SI/consultancy (Accenture, Deloitte, Slalom, Capgemini, Infosys) selling services across many clients' tech stacks [1][2][3][4][14] | Employed by the product company itself | By construction of the postings gathered — none of the consulting-firm postings mention single-vendor exclusivity; several explicitly list multi-cloud/multi-platform work (Deloitte: "Mule 4.9, Java 21, AMIs, Kubernetes, Elastic Search" in one posting) [2] |
| Comp structure | Base + modest bonus (~6-15% of base observed) [12][13], no/rare equity below partner | Typically includes larger equity/RSU components common to product companies (not independently re-verified for AWS/Salesforce SA in this pass — carried over as background knowledge, flagged as a gap) | Partial — consulting side is well evidenced [12][13]; vendor-side comp comparison not independently re-confirmed in this research pass |
| Hybrid/edge case: AWS Professional Services | AWS ProServe SAs are AWS employees but explicitly run consulting-style engagements: "owning technical engagements," "acting as a trusted advisor," "experience in consulting business and working at the CxO level" required, and "required to travel to client locations and deliver professional services" | — | [15]. This is a genuine edge case: same employer as a pure vendor SA, but engagement style, client-facing cadence, and even required "consulting business" experience mirror the consulting-side archetype far more than a single-product field SA role. Recommend flagging explicitly as hybrid in the taxonomy rather than forcing it into either bucket. |

### vs. Forward-Deployed Engineer (FDE)

Strong, direct, well-sourced contrast, primarily from a single comparison source cross-referenced against Palantir-specific sources:

- **Deliverable / ownership:** "A solutions architect designs the implementation plan, and a forward deployed engineer builds and owns the production solution" — SA "owns the design that gets handed off to someone else to run," FDE "owns the customer outcome in production" [16].
- **Coding intensity:** FDE spends "70 to 90%" of the week writing production code; SA "codes mainly to prototype and prove technical fit," with coding "not the bulk of the job" [16]. This is the single clearest, most quantified distinguishing data point found in this research pass.
- **Timing in client lifecycle:** SA engages "from late pre-sale through implementation, often before contracts are finalized"; FDE engages "post-sale, during rollout and early operations for strategic accounts" [16].
- **Engagement depth/duration (client count):** Palantir's FDE model ("Deltas") places engineers as "part of a team that directly supports one customer," focused on "technology-driven value creation: deploying and customizing Palantir platforms" for that one account long-term [17][18] — i.e., one deep, long-term embed. This contrasts with the consulting SA pattern of a portfolio of concurrent/sequential client engagements implied by utilization-rate-based staffing models [6]. Direct one-to-one client-count comparison between FDE and consulting SA was not found as an explicit stated ratio in any single source — this specific "one embed vs. portfolio" framing is a reasonable synthesis across sources [16][17][18] rather than a single directly-cited statistic, and should be treated as strong-but-synthesized evidence, not a verbatim sourced claim.
- **Comp:** FDE reported at $350K–$750K at top-tier firms vs. SA $250K–$450K "with heavier OTE variability," per the same comparison source [16]. Directionally consistent with FDE roles (Palantir-style) commanding a premium tied to scarcity and production ownership, though this specific number pair comes from one aggregator-style source and was not independently cross-checked against a second source in this pass — flag as single-sourced.
- **Career track divergence:** "SA track runs toward architecture and pre-sales leadership... FDE track runs toward product, deployment leadership, and founding" [16][17].

### Net assessment for the split-vs-merge decision

- **Consulting-side SA vs. vendor-side SA:** Evidence supports a genuine, if narrower-than-expected, distinction — primarily breadth-of-platform and employer/engagement-model, with comp structure showing suggestive but incompletely verified differences. The AWS Professional Services case is real evidence that the boundary is porous: a vendor-employed SA doing consulting-style, multi-engagement, advisory work looks more like this archetype than like a single-product field SA. **Recommendation for the stakeholder: keep as separate archetypes, but explicitly document AWS ProServe (and analogous vendor professional-services orgs, e.g., Microsoft, Salesforce, Google Cloud consulting arms) as a documented hybrid/edge case in the assessment rather than pretending the line is clean.**
- **Consulting-side SA vs. FDE:** Evidence here is comparatively stronger and more quantified (coding % of week, deliverable ownership, client-count/embed-depth pattern, comp bands) than the vendor-side comparison, but draws heavily on a single comparison article [16] rather than triangulated primary sources for the FDE side. Palantir-specific sources [17][18] corroborate the core "one deep embed, own production" pattern independently, which strengthens confidence. **Recommendation: keep as separate archetypes; the coding-intensity and single-vs-portfolio-client distinctions are the most defensible, citable differentiators to lead with in assessment copy.**
- **Overall:** the taxonomy split is evidence-supported, not merely assumed — but the comp and exit-path sections in particular rely on thinner sourcing (see Open Questions) and a second research pass specifically on vendor-side SA comp and FDE primary-source content (e.g., a first-person Palantir Delta blog post, not just secondary comparison articles) would materially strengthen the citation base before finalizing.

## 10. Open questions / notes

- **Entry paths** are the weakest-evidenced section — no source directly surveys "typical prior title before becoming a consulting SA" (e.g., % from software engineering, % from campus/MBA hire, % from client-side IT). Current entry-path claims are inferred from posting qualification lists, not direct survey/statistical data. Needs a follow-up pass (e.g., LinkedIn "About" bios of current consulting SAs, or a practitioner career-path retrospective post).
- **Exit paths** are essentially unevidenced in this pass — flagged as a gap, not populated with unsourced claims. A follow-up search specifically for "former Accenture/Deloitte Solutions Architect now [role]" LinkedIn bios or a consulting-exit-opportunities survey (e.g., Management Consulted, MConsultingPrep exit-ops content) would help.
- **Utilization-linked bonus causal claim (Section 3):** supported by general consulting-compensation literature but not by a direct primary-source company document (e.g., an actual Accenture or Deloitte compensation policy page) confirming the bonus formula includes a utilization variable by name.
